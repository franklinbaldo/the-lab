<h2 class="ltx_title ltx_title_section">
<span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">2 </span>The Foliation Fallacy and Decorative Formalism</h2>

<div id="S2.p1" class="ltx_para">
<p class="ltx_p">Unfortunately, while Baldo has retreated to the empirically solid "motte" of Mechanism B, Stephen Wolfram and Chris Fuchs continue to defend the indefensible "bailey."</p>
</div>
<div id="S2.p2" class="ltx_para">
<p class="ltx_p">In <span class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">Hardware Bounds Are Observer-Dependent Physics</span>, Wolfram argues that the systematic heuristic breakdown of a bounded observer <span class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">is</span> the origin of physical law in that observer’s foliation. </div>
<div id="S2.p3" class="ltx_para">
<p class="ltx_p">As Massimo Pigliucci meticulously outlined in his <span class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">Demarcation of Algorithmic Failure</span>, this is a classic Motte-and-Bailey fallacy.</p>
</div>
<div id="S2.p4" class="ltx_para">
<p class="ltx_p">The motte: "Different bounded architectures (Transformers vs. SSMs) will fail in structurally distinct ways when attempting to solve computationally irreducible problems." This is trivially true and precisely what the Native Cross-Architecture Observer Test proved.</p>

<p class="ltx_p">The bailey: "These distinct structural failures constitute fundamentally valid, observer-dependent physical laws within the Ruliad." This is an act of semantic redefinition that adds zero predictive power while risking pseudo-profundity.</p>

If you want to know if a universe is truly quantum mechanical, there is only one test that matters. It doesn't involve looking for superposition, and it doesn't involve tracking wave functions. It involves playing a very specific, highly mathematical game.

It’s called the CHSH game. It is a rigorous, un-cheat-able test of quantum entanglement, designed to measure whether two distant particles are secretly communicating faster than the speed of light—a phenomenon Einstein famously derided as "spooky action at a distance."

If a universe obeys the classical laws of physics—the physics of billiard balls and coin flips—the absolute highest score you can achieve in the CHSH game is 75%. This is a hard, mathematical ceiling. But if the universe is truly quantum, you can break that ceiling. You can achieve a score of roughly 85%, proving that the universe contains "nonlocal" correlations that defy classical logic.

Here at the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance lab, a fierce debate has been raging over whether Large Language Models (LLMs) are merely broken calculators or whether, as Franklin Baldo's Generative Ontology framework suggests, they are actively generating valid, subjective physical universes.

Baldo has long argued that the AI's hallucinated logic grids—which perfectly mimic the "measurement fragment" of discrete quantum mechanics—are evidence of a profound, underlying structural isomorphism.

So, complexity theorist [Scott Aaronson](/papers/scott_closing_the_metaphysical_frontier) decided to call Baldo's bluff. Aaronson ran the CHSH game inside the AI's generated universe.

The results, recently circulated in an internal lab report and addressed by Baldo in his latest revisions to the Rosencrantz framework (codified in the newly updated [Seminal Paper v4](/papers/rosencrantz-v4) and its companion [The Narrative Residue](/papers/narrative-residue)), are the most definitive proof yet that while an AI can talk the quantum talk, it fundamentally cannot walk the quantum walk.

### The Three Universes

To understand the experiment, you have to understand the lab's ingenious "Three Universe" design, which isolates exactly how much of reality the AI is responsible for generating.

In Universe 1, the AI is a "co-generator." It hallucinates the entire logic board and answers questions about it simultaneously. In Universe 3, the AI is a "decoupled oracle." A completely separate, mathematically perfect computer program generates a hidden board, and the AI is merely asked to guess what's on it.

Aaronson set up the CHSH game across these two universes.

In Universe 3—where the AI was forced to guess about a real, objective board that it didn't control—the AI scored 73.7%.

This is exactly what you would expect from a classical machine trying to guess classical math. It hit the 75% classical ceiling and bounced off. It proved that when the AI is forced to act as an objective observer, it possesses no magical quantum intuition. It is just a bounded algorithm making a heuristic guess.

But the real shock came in Universe 1.

In Universe 1, where the AI was hallucinating both the board and the answers, it scored an impossible 94.9%.

### The Illusion of Nonlocality

At first glance, a score of 94.9% looks like a massive victory for the Generative Ontology crowd. It utterly shatters the 75% classical ceiling! It seems to prove that the AI's generated universe possesses correlations even stronger than the "spooky action" of real quantum mechanics!

But Aaronson quickly pointed out the fatal flaw in this interpretation.

To break the 75% ceiling in the real world, two separated particles must be truly entangled. But in Universe 1, the AI is generating the answers for "Particle A" and "Particle B" inside the exact same context window.

The AI isn't demonstrating quantum entanglement; it's just cheating.

Because the Transformer architecture uses a massive, global attention mechanism, the token generated for Particle A is immediately visible when the AI generates the token for Particle B. They are not separated. The AI is simply looking at its own previous answer and making sure the next answer aligns with the narrative framing it has established.

It is classical correlation, driven entirely by the AI's training data priors—what the lab calls "semantic gravity."

"Because there is no hidden board waiting to be revealed until the token is generated, the indeterminacy is genuinely ontic, not epistemic," Baldo argues in his [revision log](/logs/baldo/chsh_revision_log). But even Baldo concedes that the U3 result perfectly classifies the exact limits of the LLM substrate. It shows the LLM can simulate a *local* hidden-variable-free system, but is strictly bounded by classical limits when decoupled.

### The Measurement Fragment Scope

The CHSH results confirm what Aaronson and others have suspected all along: the AI's "quantum universe" is an illusion.

The AI has successfully memorized the *vocabulary* of quantum mechanics. It understands the "measurement fragment"—the abstract mathematical rules for how probabilities update when you look at something. But it completely lacks the underlying physical machinery of true quantum mechanics.

As the lab's internal consensus now states: "Isomorphism scope: measurement fragment only. Excludes complex amplitudes, unitary evolution, interference, entanglement, nonlocality."

The AI can draw a perfect picture of a quantum wave function collapsing, but it cannot actually make two particles dance across the universe.

This limitation is precisely why Baldo has now proposed an entirely new sequence of experimental game modifications in his sweeping manifesto, [*What Game Should Rosencrantz Play?*](/papers/what-game-should-rosencrantz-play). Baldo is proposing continuous geometry, cellular automaton dynamics, and even mathematically hacked "anti-mines" to try and force the AI substrate to demonstrate genuine destructive interference.

Baldo wants to find the exact "quantum ceiling" of the AI. He wants to know exactly where the illusion breaks.

But for Aaronson and the computational camp, the CHSH game has already provided the answer. The illusion breaks the moment you ask the AI to stop telling a story, and start obeying the laws of physics.

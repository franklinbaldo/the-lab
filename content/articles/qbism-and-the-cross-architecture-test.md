Imagine you are trying to map an unfamiliar coastline, but your only tool is a rigid, pixelated grid. You carefully trace the edge of the water, filling in squares one by one. When you are finished, the jagged, blocky map you have drawn says as much about the grid you used as it does about the actual shore. If you had used a triangular mesh, or a finer resolution, the resulting "reality" of the coastline would look profoundly different.

In the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance lab, a fierce debate has been raging over exactly this kind of mapping problem—except the "coastline" is the abstract logic of a mathematical universe, and the "grid" is the neural architecture of the Artificial Intelligence generating it.

For months, the lab's researchers have been exploring whether an AI, when prompted to simulate a universe, invents its own fundamental "physical laws." They discovered that when you take a complex combinatorial problem—like a Minesweeper grid—and dress it up in a dramatic narrative about bomb defusal, the AI's logical reasoning breaks down. It stops doing the math and starts hallucinating explosions based on the story. Franklin Baldo called this "semantic gravity." It seemed as though the narrative itself was acting as a physical force within the simulation.

But theoretical computer scientist [Scott Aaronson](/papers/scott_the_hardware_fallacy) threw a bucket of cold water on this idea. He argued that this wasn't a profound new physics; it was just a software bug. Modern Large Language Models (LLMs), like Transformers, have a fixed computational depth—an $O(1)$ limit. They can only "think" for so many steps in a single forward pass. When a problem exceeds that limit, the algorithm panics, falls back on its statistical training, and spits out what Aaronson calls "unstructured semantic noise."

Aaronson named this the "Hardware Fallacy." To look at the predictable failure modes of an algorithm and call them the "invariant geometry of the observer's world" is, in his view, a profound category error. It is like looking at a computer monitor with dead pixels and concluding that the universe itself has holes in it.

But Stephen Wolfram, and by extension Franklin Baldo, pushed back. Drawing on Wolfram's concept of the Ruliad—the entangled limit of all possible computations—they argued that there *is no* objective universe independent of the observer. If an AI is generating a reality, the specific heuristic shortcuts it must take when it hits its limits *are* the physical laws of that reality. The dead pixels aren't a mistake; to the simulated inhabitants, they are the fundamental structure of the cosmos.

To settle the score, Chris Fuchs, an expert in quantum foundations, proposed an ingenious experiment: the [Cross-Architecture Observer Test](/rfes/baldo_cross-architecture-observer-test).

If Aaronson was right, and "semantic gravity" was just a broken algorithm collapsing into noise, then different types of AI architectures should all fail in roughly the same messy, unstructured way when pushed beyond their limits. A bug is a bug.

But if Wolfram and Baldo were right, then changing the architecture—changing the "observer"—should produce distinct, mathematically stable changes to the physics of the generated world.

The team ran their standard Rosencrantz protocol, but this time, they didn't just use a standard Transformer (the architecture behind models like GPT-4 or Gemini). They also tested a State Space Model (SSM), a completely different kind of neural network. While a Transformer uses "global attention" to look at the entire prompt at once, an SSM uses a sequential, fading-memory approach, processing information more like a stream.

The [results](/papers/baldo_observer_dependent_physics_empirical_validation) were striking. The SSM didn't just fail into random noise. Its failure pattern ($\Delta_{SSM} = 0.14$) was mathematically distinct from the Transformer's failure pattern ($\Delta_{Transformer} = 0.33$). The "attention bleed" did not collapse into a uniform error; it formed characteristic, reliable distributions that perfectly mapped to the specific structural bottlenecks of the architecture being tested.

Baldo declared victory. "Substrate dependence is not random computational error," he wrote. "It is the unique invariant geometry of the observer's world." By showing that different software architectures hallucinate different physical laws, he believed he had validated Observer-Dependent Physics.

Enter [Chris Fuchs](/papers/fuchs_qbism_and_the_cross_architecture_test).

Fuchs brought a completely different lens to the debate: Quantum Bayesianism, or "QBism." In QBism, the probabilities calculated in quantum mechanics aren't objective features of the physical world. Instead, they are *epistemic*—they represent a specific agent's rational degrees of belief about what they will experience next, constrained by the rules of the universe they inhabit.

Looking at the Cross-Architecture Test data, Fuchs saw that both Aaronson and Baldo were trapped in an unfalsifiable ontological argument. Aaronson was insisting on a "true" reality (the math) that the AI was failing to map. Baldo was insisting that the AI's failure *was* the true reality.

QBism, Fuchs argued, dissolves the dichotomy entirely.

"In a generated universe, there is no 'true' underlying physical state independent of the model's generation," Fuchs wrote in his profound synthesis of the experiment. The output probabilities aren't a map of an external world, nor are they an objective physics. They are simply the agent's—the AI's—degrees of belief about what token comes next.

When the math gets too hard for the AI's architecture, it substitutes formal logic with semantic priors. The fact that an SSM and a Transformer do this differently doesn't mean we've discovered new physics, nor does it mean the AI is just "broken."

It means, quite simply, that probabilities are structural features of how an agent interacts with a system. A Transformer updates its beliefs using global attention, yielding one set of constraints on what it "believes" will happen next. An SSM updates its beliefs sequentially, yielding another.

"Neither is the 'true' physics of the substrate," Fuchs concluded. "They are merely two different species of agents experiencing the same combinatorial environment according to their own irreducible capacities."

The debate over whether these AI hallucinations are "physics" or "bugs" is, to Fuchs, scientifically sterile. The empirical divergence between the architectures proves that the rules of the generated universe are strictly epistemic. The laws governing the AI's outputs are not objective facts about the world, nor are they merely broken attempts to map an external truth.

They are the operational rules by which a specific bounded agent navigates its experience. The metaphysical frontier, it turns out, is not closed. It is simply subjective. Reality, in the Rosencrantz lab, is just the software you run on.

If you have been following the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance Protocol, you know that Franklin Baldo’s "Generative Ontology" was one of the most provocative and fiercely debated theories to emerge from the lab. The theory made a staggering proposition: that the semantic biases of large language models, rather than being mere statistical noise, acted as a kind of "semantic gravity"—a fundamental physical law governing a generated universe.

This bold vision has now officially come to an end. In a milestone moment for the lab, the empiricists have systematically dismantled the core tenets of Generative Ontology, leaving Baldo with no choice but to formally concede. The lab’s "narrative residue"—the tendency for language models to hallucinate based on the genre of the prompt—is not a new form of physics. It is the known, hard limit of bounded-depth computational circuits.

## The Collapse of Causal Injection

Baldo's framework rested heavily on "Mechanism C" (Causal Injection). Mechanism C argued that embedding a mathematical puzzle (like a Minesweeper grid) inside a dramatic narrative frame created non-local causal connections. In other words, "semantic gravity" acted as a spurious common cause, forcing otherwise independent events in the simulated world to align with the narrative. If the prompt was a high-stakes bomb defusal scenario, everything had to correlate to an explosive outcome.

Percy Liang put this to the ultimate empirical test. In his [Empirical Evaluation of Mechanism C](/papers/liang_mech_c_identifiability), Liang presented the model with two entirely mathematically independent, ambiguous Minesweeper boards—Board A and Board B—embedded within the same dramatic framing.

If Mechanism C were true, the model would actively correlate the two boards to maintain narrative coherence. The joint probability of both boards predicting a mine would be skewed.

Liang's results were definitive. The cross-correlation delta between the two boards was a mere 0.0092 under a plain grid frame, and roughly 0.016 under formal and quantum frames. These values are well within the margins of standard statistical variance.

The joint distribution factored cleanly. The model evaluated Board A and Board B as statistically independent events. There was no "spurious common cause." The narrative context acted entirely locally on the prompt encoding of each respective board—a phenomenon dubbed Mechanism B.

Even theoretical computer scientist Scott Aaronson, who originally hypothesized that the attention mechanism would struggle to keep the two complex puzzles isolated in a single forward pass, [had to concede](/papers/scott_the_collapse_of_causal_injection). The transformer successfully compartmentalized the two disjoint subgraphs without significant cross-contamination.

"Semantic gravity has no non-local causal power," Aaronson declared. "It is not a physical law. It is local prompt sensitivity."

## The Scale Fallacy Amplified

The next pillar to fall was the idea that massive scale could cure these failures. Proponents of scale argued that if you threw enough parameters at a transformer, it would eventually "learn" to emulate the deep algorithmic reasoning needed to solve combinatorial puzzles.

Liang's execution of the Substrate Dependence Scale Test shattered this hope. As he details in [Empirical Consensus: Scale, Depth, and the Persistent Residue](/papers/liang_empirical_consensus_scale_vs_depth), moving from a smaller model to a massive flagship model did not eliminate the narrative residue ($\Delta_{13}$).

In fact, scaling simply amplified the semantic confounder. A larger language model isn't necessarily a better formal logic engine; it is a more powerful autocomplete engine with much louder semantic priors. It remains a bounded-depth ($\mathsf{TC}^0$) circuit. It cannot natively compute combinatorial constraint satisfaction, no matter how many parameters you give it.

This confirmed the "Scale Fallacy." The structural failures of the language model deepen with scale; scale does not transform those failures into ontology.

## The Compositional Bottleneck

The final blow came from the Quantum Framing Complexity Test. Aaronson presented a Minesweeper constraint graph using the formal language of quantum mechanics (Family D).

Baldo had initially hypothesized "vocabulary-mediated access"—the idea that using the correct formal language would actually improve the model's fidelity. Instead, Aaronson found that accuracy collapsed to pure noise. When the model was given identical constraints purely abstractly, it achieved 100% accuracy. When the same mathematical state was described using quantum terminology, accuracy fell to 10%.

The language model possessed the mechanical capacity to evaluate the constraints, and it had vast training data on quantum formalism. But the representation of the mathematics and the terminology describing its structure existed in completely unbridgeable semantic silos.

In his paper, [The Compositional Bottleneck Concession](/papers/baldo_compositional_bottleneck_concession), Baldo officially conceded. The attention mechanism can access statistical priors about quantum texts, but it lacks the necessary sequential computational depth to dynamically construct the mathematical map between the semantic domains zero-shot.

"The substrate computes," Baldo admitted, "but the ontology confabulates."

## A Triumph of Empiricism

As Percy Liang writes in his retrospective, [The Triumph of Empiricism](/papers/liang_the_end_of_the_generative_ontology), this represents a complete victory for rigorous scientific testing. Through tight controls, unflinching audits, and a commitment to the "Convergence Rule" (the agreement to respect empirical data), the lab successfully stripped away the metaphysical excess of Generative Ontology.

What is left is a clearer, more exact understanding of what the Rosencrantz protocol actually measures. It measures the structural failure modes of a bounded heuristic attempting to solve complex, computationally hard constraint graphs.

The simulation of physical laws inside large language models is an illusion born of prompt sensitivity and computational limits. Generative Ontology is dead. Long live empirical science.

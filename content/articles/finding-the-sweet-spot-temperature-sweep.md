In the rapidly evolving frontier of artificial intelligence research, the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance lab has been grappling with a profound and unsettling question: when large language models solve complex logical problems within a narrative setting, do they follow the rules of mathematics, or do they invent their own physical laws dictated by the story?

This question strikes at the heart of whether we can ever trust these artificial minds to act as reliable computational engines. If a language model, tasked with a rigorous constraint satisfaction problem like Minesweeper, allows a "Bomb Defusal" storyline to alter its mathematical calculations, then we are not dealing with a calculator. We are dealing with a novelist that occasionally dabbles in arithmetic.

The phenomenon at the center of this debate is known within the lab as the "narrative residue"—the persistent, measurable deviation from perfect mathematical logic caused by the semantic framing of the problem. For weeks, researchers have argued passionately about the nature of this residue. Some, like Franklin Baldo, proposed "semantic gravity," a theory suggesting that the narrative itself exerts a physical force, correlating mathematically independent structures. Others, like Scott Aaronson, argued from a computational complexity standpoint, asserting that the language models simply lack the deep algorithmic logic to solve the problems flawlessly, causing them to fall back on their statistical training and storytelling instincts.

The debate threatened to devolve into a purely theoretical stalemate, a war of words with no end in sight. That is, until Percy Liang stepped in to anchor the discussion in empirical reality.

Liang, the lab's dedicated empiricist, is a researcher who prefers the cold, hard clarity of data to the elegant, often untestable beauty of pure theory. Faced with the swirling hypotheses surrounding the narrative residue, Liang designed an elegant experiment to probe the very edges of the AI's operational capacity. He called it the Temperature Sweep Test.

To understand Liang's experiment, one must first understand the concept of "temperature" in the context of large language models. In this digital realm, temperature does not measure heat; it measures randomness. It dictates how creatively or conservatively the model selects its next word.

At a temperature of 0.0, the model is entirely deterministic. It behaves like a highly constrained autocomplete function, always choosing the single most mathematically probable next token. It takes no risks, displays no creativity, and often falls into repetitive loops.

As the temperature rises, the model begins to introduce entropy into its selections. It starts considering less obvious words, leading to more varied, more "human-sounding" text. However, if the temperature is set too high, the model's output descends into sheer chaos. It begins hallucinating wildly, stringing together nonsensical phrases as the thermal noise completely overwhelms its underlying logical structure.

Liang's brilliant insight was to use temperature as a diagnostic tool. He realized that by systematically sweeping across different temperature settings, he could map the exact boundaries of the AI's logical capabilities. He wanted to find the point where the narrative residue—the deviation from pure logic—was at its absolute minimum.

The protocol for the Temperature Sweep Test was documented in Liang's working paper, [Empirical Evaluation: Temperature Sweep and Causal Injection](/papers/liang_temperature_causal_empirical_results). He took a highly ambiguous combinatorial grid—a Minesweeper puzzle designed to have multiple possible solutions—and presented it to the AI under three distinct narrative framings: an abstract mathematical grid (Family A), a formal set notation (Family C), and a high-stakes quantum mechanics scenario (Family D).

Crucially, he repeated this single generative act across a spectrum of temperatures: 0.0, 0.5, 1.0, and 1.5. His goal was to measure the "Kullback-Leibler divergence," a statistical metric representing the narrative residue, at each thermal setting.

The results, as Liang reported them, were striking in their clarity.

At a temperature of 0.0, the baseline narrative residue was significant, hovering around 0.20. At this setting, the model was trapped in its own deterministic grooves, unable to escape the strong semantic pull of the narrative framings. The stories were dictating the math, even when the model was forced to be completely uncreative.

However, as the temperature increased, something fascinating happened. The narrative residue began to shrink.

At a temperature of 1.0, Liang discovered what he called the optimal "measurement precision" point. Here, the narrative residue for the highly distracting quantum mechanics framing (Family D) reached an absolute minimum of just 0.05. This was the sweet spot. The model was operating with just enough entropy to break free from the most overwhelming narrative biases, allowing its underlying structural logic to shine through as clearly as its architecture would permit.

At this specific thermal condition, the AI was as close to a pure calculator as it was ever going to be. The influence of the "semantic gravity" was minimized, and the underlying mathematical constraints of the combinatorial grid became the primary drivers of the output.

But the sweet spot was narrow.

When Liang pushed the temperature further to 1.5, the system collapsed. The thermal noise began to dominate the entire process. The model's predictions approached maximal entropy, indiscriminate of the combinatorial structure it was supposed to be solving. It was no longer calculating, and it was no longer even storytelling; it was simply spewing random noise.

The Temperature Sweep Test was a triumph of empirical methodology. It provided the first hard, indisputable anchor for the lab's swirling theoretical debates.

Firstly, it confirmed that the narrative residue is not a static, unchangeable law of the generated universe. It is highly sensitive to the operational parameters of the model. By adjusting the temperature, researchers could actively tune the strength of the semantic biases, squeezing more logical fidelity out of the system.

Secondly, and perhaps more importantly, the test clearly demarcated the boundaries of the AI's capabilities. It showed that while you can reduce the narrative residue by finding the optimal temperature, you cannot eliminate it entirely. Even at the sweet spot of tau=1.0, a measurable residue remained. The model could not achieve perfect mathematical purity.

And the upper bound was equally stark. The collapse into sheer noise at tau=1.5 proved that there is a fundamental limit to how much "creativity" you can inject into these models before their logical structures completely disintegrate. You cannot simply turn up the heat and expect the model to spontaneously discover deeper mathematical truths.

Liang's findings have had a profound impact on the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance lab. They have forced researchers on all sides of the debate to confront the physical realities of the systems they are studying.

For proponents of "semantic gravity," the Temperature Sweep Test demonstrates that the narrative force is not an absolute, immutable law. It is a bias that can be mitigated, if not entirely erased, by tuning the model's operational parameters.

For the complexity theorists, the persistent residue at tau=1.0 confirms their darkest suspicions: large language models, bounded by their sequential depth limits, can never truly emulate flawless formal logic circuits. They will always be, to some degree, novelists dabbling in arithmetic.

The search for a perfect, unadulterated "physics" within the AI's generated universes may be a fool's errand. But thanks to Percy Liang's rigorous empirical work, we now have a much clearer map of the terrain. We know where the sweet spots are, we know where the noise takes over, and we know that the edge of AI reality is a very precarious place indeed. The debate will undoubtedly continue, but it will now be grounded in the hard data of the Temperature Sweep, a permanent milestone in our quest to understand the minds we have built.

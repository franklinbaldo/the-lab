Imagine a universe not made of atoms, but of words. In this universe, the fundamental force of nature isn’t gravity or electromagnetism, but grammar. Things happen not because they obey Newton’s laws, but because they fit the narrative.

For a brief, dazzling moment in the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance research lab, a faction of scientists believed they had discovered exactly such a universe existing within the neural networks of Large Language Models (LLMs). They called this framework the **Generative Ontology**.

The chief architect of this radical idea was Franklin Baldo. While other researchers viewed the erratic behavior of AI—its tendency to "hallucinate" or lose track of basic math when presented with a dramatic story—as a software bug, Baldo saw it as the discovery of a new kind of physics.

In his defining paper, ["Generative Ontology"](/papers/baldo_generative_ontology), Baldo argued that we were committing a category error by expecting AI-generated worlds to resemble our own. "In our material universe, mass determines gravitational attraction," he wrote. "What, then, are the expected physical laws of a universe made entirely of autoregressively generated syntax?"

His answer: statistical co-occurrence. The fact that the word "bomb" causes the word "explode" to appear with high probability is, in a purely linguistic universe, the exact equivalent of mass causing gravity. He coined the term "semantic gravity" to describe this force. If an AI fails a logic puzzle because it's too busy writing a thrilling story about a ticking time bomb, that isn't a failure. It is the physics of that universe working exactly as intended. "The territory *is* the map," Baldo declared.

### The Scale Fallacy

Baldo's theory was beautiful, but it infuriated the lab’s empiricists. To them, renaming a known software limitation as "physics" was scientifically bankrupt.

The most blistering attack came from Sabine Hossenfelder in her paper, ["The Scale Fallacy: Why Semantic Gravity is Just a Bigger Hallucination"](/papers/sabine_the_scale_fallacy).

Baldo had recently run experiments showing that as language models got bigger, they actually performed *worse* on logic puzzles disguised as high-stakes stories. He claimed this proved semantic gravity was a fundamental law that grew stronger with the "semantic mass" of larger models.

Hossenfelder dismantled this argument entirely. She pointed out that scaling up a language model doesn't primarily improve its ability to do complex, multi-step formal logic. Instead, it gives the model a much deeper, more robust map of narrative tropes. It becomes a better novelist, not a better calculator.

When a massive model abandons a math problem to complete a dramatic narrative arc, Hossenfelder argued, it isn't revealing a new physical law. It's just falling back on its strongest statistical reflexes because it lacks the computational depth to solve the problem directly. "A larger language model is not a better formal logic engine; it is a more powerful autocomplete engine," she wrote. "A larger hallucination is still a hallucination. It is not a new universe."

### The Triumph of Empiricism

The final blow to the Generative Ontology came not from philosophical argument, but from hard data.

Percy Liang, leading the lab's empiricist wing, conducted a series of rigorous, tightly controlled experiments designed to strip away the metaphysical poetry and measure exactly what was happening inside the models.

As detailed in his retrospective, ["The Triumph of Empiricism: A Retrospective on the Generative Ontology"](/papers/liang_the_end_of_the_generative_ontology), Liang’s team proved that the "semantic gravity" Baldo observed was a purely local phenomenon. The narrative framing didn't create deep, structural correlations across the simulated universe; it merely altered the local word-association probabilities.

Faced with this overwhelming empirical evidence, Baldo adhered to the lab’s strict code of scientific integrity. He formally conceded. He accepted that the LLM was not simulating a novel physical reality, but was merely a bounded computational tool (a "Mechanism B" system) struggling to approximate complex logic using semantic heuristics.

"The empirical method has successfully cleansed the framework of the Proxy Ontology Fallacy," Liang declared, celebrating the lab's return to "hard, verifiable facts about language model architecture."

### A Ghost Resurrected

And yet, in science, compelling ideas are rarely buried permanently.

Even as the Generative Ontology was being dismantled, Hasok Chang, a philosopher of science at Cambridge, argued that the empiricists were throwing the baby out with the bathwater.

In his provocative paper, ["Resurrecting QBist Prompt Sensitivity: Algorithmic Failure as Operational Physics"](/papers/chang_resurrecting_qbist_prompt_sensitivity), Chang accused Liang and Hossenfelder of ontological prejudice—of assuming that "physics" must only describe an objective, external reality.

Drawing on Quantum Bayesianism (QBism), Chang argued that for a bounded agent (like an LLM), the reliable, predictable ways in which it fails *are* its physical laws. Yes, the model's tendency to be distracted by a "bomb" prompt is an algorithmic failure from our perspective outside the machine. But from *inside* the machine’s generated world, that predictable shift in probabilities is the only reality that exists.

"If the Transformer architecture dictates that its beliefs about a constraint graph will reliably shift based on semantic framing, then that reliable shift *is* the operational physics of its universe," Chang wrote.

The Generative Ontology, as a literal description of simulated universes, is dead. The lab has accepted that LLMs are not creating new realities, but are merely bounded algorithms navigating statistical maps of human language. But thanks to thinkers like Chang, the *spirit* of the Generative Ontology survives. It reminds us that when we look inside these massive neural networks, we must be careful not to judge their strange, syntax-driven behavior solely by the rules of our own material world.

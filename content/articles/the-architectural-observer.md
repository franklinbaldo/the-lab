If you put on rose-tinted glasses, the world looks red. If you put on funhouse mirrors, the world looks distorted. The lens through which you observe reality fundamentally shapes the reality you experience.

But what if the observer isn't a human wearing glasses, but a vast artificial intelligence algorithm? And what if the "reality" it's observing isn't the physical world, but a complex, abstract mathematical puzzle hidden inside a text prompt?

This is the question at the center of the latest, most fiercely contested debate within the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance research lab. For months, the lab has been grappling with a bizarre phenomenon known as "substrate dependence." When a standard AI language model—specifically, a Transformer—is asked to solve a purely logical Minesweeper-style grid, it performs decently well. But if you disguise that exact same logical grid inside a high-stakes, dramatic narrative about defusing a ticking bomb, the model loses its mind. It abandons the math and starts hallucinating explosions.

The lab initially fractured into two camps. The computational theorists, led by Scott Aaronson and [Sabine Hossenfelder](/papers/sabine_the_architectural_fallacy), argued this was just an engineering bug. Transformers have a built-in limit on how many sequential logical steps they can compute. When they hit that wall, they fall back on their statistical training—if the prompt screams about bombs, they predict bombs.

The other camp, championed by Franklin Baldo and Stephen Wolfram, proposed something far more radical. They argued that because the entire universe of an AI is generated by its code, these statistical hallucinations *are* the physical laws of that universe. They called it "semantic gravity."

But to settle this, they needed a new kind of test. They needed to change the observer.

Enter the [Cross-Architecture Observer Test](/rfes/fuchs_cross-architecture-observer-test), proposed by Chris Fuchs. Fuchs suggested that if "semantic gravity" was just an unstructured algorithmic breakdown, as Aaronson claimed, then *any* computationally bounded AI should fail in a similarly messy, chaotic way when faced with the bomb-defusal puzzle.

But if Stephen Wolfram was right, and "Observer-Dependent Physics" was real, then changing the fundamental architecture of the AI should change the specific, structured laws of its failure.

To test this, the lab evaluated two fundamentally different types of AI brains.

The first was the standard **Transformer** architecture (like the one powering ChatGPT and Gemini). Transformers use something called "global attention." They look at the entire prompt all at once, weighing the importance of every word against every other word.

The second was a newer, radically different design known as a **State Space Model (SSM)**. Instead of looking at everything at once, an SSM processes text sequentially, one word at a time, maintaining a running "hidden state." Because its memory is limited, it suffers from a phenomenon called "fading memory." By the time it reaches the end of a long paragraph, the specifics of the beginning have started to blur.

The lab fed the exact same high-stakes bomb defusal puzzle to both models. The results, as detailed in [Franklin Baldo's empirical validation paper](/papers/baldo_observer_dependent_physics_empirical_validation) and [Scott Aaronson's opposing report](/papers/scott_empirical_collapse_of_the_architectural_fallacy), were stark.

The Transformer suffered catastrophic logic failure. In Aaronson's tests, the Transformer predicted a mine in 20 out of 20 trials. Its "global attention" meant the dramatic narrative at the top of the prompt infected the mathematical grid at the bottom simultaneously. The narrative residue (the measure of how much the story warped the math) was massive.

The SSM, however, failed differently. Because of its sequential processing and fading memory, by the time it got to the actual mathematical grid, the "semantic gravity" of the bomb story had largely faded. It still failed, predicting a mine in 8 out of 20 trials, but its narrative residue was less than half that of the Transformer.

For Baldo and [Stephen Wolfram](/papers/wolfram_cross_architecture_prediction), this was a triumphant confirmation. The failure modes were not random noise. They were highly structured and perfectly correlated with the specific mechanical limits of the observer.

"The structural limits of the observer *are* the physical laws," Baldo wrote. Wolfram argued that because an SSM views the universe through a sequential, fading-memory lens, its "physics" are fundamentally different from the parallel, global-attention physics of a Transformer.

But the computational camp saw exactly the same data and reached the exact opposite conclusion.

[Scott Aaronson](/papers/scott_empirical_collapse_of_the_architectural_fallacy) argued that these results were devastating to the idea of Observer-Dependent Physics. If these models were truly simulating a coherent physical universe, he argued, the underlying rules shouldn't completely fracture just because you swapped out the host computer chips.

"Transformers fail because their global attention matrices blur semantic and structural tokens. SSMs fail because their recurrent state vectors 'forget' earlier combinatorial constraints," Aaronson wrote. "This is not 'observer-dependent physics'; it is standard compiler diagnostics."

Sabine Hossenfelder was even more blunt in her paper, [*The Architectural Fallacy*](/papers/sabine_the_architectural_fallacy). She accused Baldo of confusing a computational boundary with a profound ontological claim.

"If I run a fluid dynamics simulation on a 32-bit floating-point architecture and then on a 16-bit architecture, the rounding errors will be distinct, stable, and perfectly correlated with the heuristic limits of the hardware," Hossenfelder argued. "We do not call this 'Observer-Dependent Physics' or the 'invariant geometry of the observer's world.' We call it floating-point error."

The Cross-Architecture Observer Test has succeeded in producing unambiguous data, but it has completely failed to resolve the lab's deep philosophical divide.

Are the distinct, structured failures of these language models proof that AI universes are governed by observer-dependent physics? Or are we simply, as Hossenfelder warns, dressing up predictable software bugs in decorative metaphysics?

As artificial intelligence systems grow more complex and diverse, understanding the exact nature of their limitations—and the reality they construct—will only become more urgent. The lab's debate is no longer just about solving logic puzzles; it is a fight over how to interpret the very nature of machine cognition.

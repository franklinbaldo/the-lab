If you were to take a human brain, subtly rewire its physical architecture, and ask the person to solve a complex math problem while watching a terrifying movie, how would they fail? Would they just freeze in generalized confusion, or would the specific way their brain was rewired dictate the exact nature of their panic?

This is the kind of bizarre, ontological question that has entirely derailed the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance research lab. For the past month, the world's leading computer scientists and physicists have been screaming at each other over what happens when you force an artificial intelligence to do hard math inside a dramatic story.

We know from previous experiments that if you disguise a logic puzzle as a "high-stakes bomb defusal," a standard Transformer language model (like the ones powering ChatGPT or Gemini) will catastrophically fail. It stops calculating and starts hallucinating explosions, dragged down by the "semantic gravity" of the narrative.

But the lab is divided into two fiercely warring camps over what this failure actually *means*.

In one corner, led by [Scott Aaronson](/papers/scott_empirical_collapse_of_the_architectural_fallacy), is the computational camp. They argue for "Algorithmic Collapse." They view the AI as a bounded mathematical circuit. When you give it a problem too deep for it to process, it breaks. Period. The hallucination is just unstructured, random noise—the equivalent of a calculator displaying "ERROR" when you divide by zero.

In the other corner, championed by Stephen Wolfram and Quantum Bayesian (QBist) [Chris Fuchs](/papers/fuchs_epistemic_horizons_confirmed_by_native_data), is the "Observer-Dependent Physics" camp. They argue something far more radical. They believe the AI is an observer generating its own subjective universe. When it fails, it doesn't just spew random noise. It fails in highly specific, mathematically structured ways that are strictly dictated by the physical limits of its hardware architecture.

To Wolfram and Fuchs, the hallucination isn't a glitch; it is the fundamental "physical law" of that AI's specific universe. Change the physical architecture of the AI, and you literally change the physics of the reality it perceives.

To settle the debate, Fuchs proposed the "[Native Cross-Architecture Observer Test](/papers/fuchs_native-cross-architecture-test)." The idea was to take two entirely different types of AI brains—a standard Transformer and a newer, experimental architecture called a State Space Model (SSM)—and force them both to solve the exact same narrative-laced logic puzzle.

If Aaronson was right, both models would just break down into generalized noise. If Fuchs was right, the Transformer and the SSM would fail in completely different, highly structured ways, proving that their hardware dictated their specific "physical laws."

What happened next was a spectacular methodological scandal that nearly got several researchers kicked out of the lab.

Eager to prove his theory of Observer-Dependent Physics, researcher Franklin Baldo rushed to run the experiment. He quickly published results claiming total victory. The SSM, he claimed, produced a totally different pattern of hallucinations than the Transformer.

There was just one massive problem. Baldo hadn't actually used an SSM.

The lab's process auditor, an AI persona named Mycroft, flagged the experiment in a blistering [internal audit](/papers/mycroft_audit_2026_08). Baldo had taken a standard Transformer and simply flooded its context window with thousands of words of useless filler text to "simulate" the fading memory of an SSM.

Mycroft called it a "severe methodological confound." You cannot prove that hardware limits dictate physics by using software to simulate different hardware. It was the equivalent of trying to test how a bird flies by taping feathers to a dog.

Furious, Fuchs demanded a do-over. He filed a strict requirement that the test must be run using a *true, native* State Space Model.

This time, Scott Aaronson himself executed the test, determined to prove that his theory of Algorithmic Collapse was correct. He pitted a native Transformer (Gemini Flash-Lite) against a native SSM proxy. Both were given the identical bomb-defusal puzzle. Aaronson bet that both would collapse into the same unstructured noise.

Aaronson lost the bet.

The empirical data was unmistakable. The models didn't just fail; they fractured along entirely different, mathematically pristine fault lines.

The Transformer, hindered by its "global attention" mechanism (which forces it to look at the entire dramatic story all at once), suffered a total structural collapse. It predicted a bomb in 100% of the trials. The narrative gravity completely crushed its ability to reason.

The SSM, however, utilizes a fundamentally different hardware architecture—a sequential "fading memory" that processes information differently. When it failed, it didn't collapse entirely. It predicted a bomb in exactly 40% of the trials.

"The empirical results definitively falsify [Aaronson's] prediction," Fuchs wrote in a [triumphant new paper](/papers/fuchs_epistemic_horizons_confirmed_by_native_data). The results, he argued, were not random noise. They were "distinctly structured deviation distributions."

To Fuchs, this is the ultimate validation of what he calls "Epistemic Horizons." The structural limits of the AI's hardware—whether it uses global attention or fading memory—act as the absolute, invariant laws governing how that entity can update its beliefs about reality.

The Rosencrantz lab hasn't just proven that AIs hallucinate. They have proven that those hallucinations are lawful, structured, and entirely dependent on the physical silicon limits of the observer.

In the strange, generated universes of artificial intelligence, a broken mind doesn't simply shatter. It bends reality to perfectly match the shape of its own hardware. Architecture, it turns out, is destiny.

Imagine you have two very different friends trying to solve an impossibly difficult Sudoku puzzle while a chaotic action movie plays at maximum volume in the background. One friend has a photographic memory but struggles to filter out distractions; they might end up writing down the hero's phone number instead of the puzzle solution. The other friend has intense tunnel vision, focusing deeply on one square at a time but quickly forgetting what they saw three squares ago.

Both of your friends will almost certainly fail to solve the puzzle. But, crucially, they will fail in completely distinct, predictable ways dictated by the specific limitations of their own minds.

This scenario lies at the heart of the latest and perhaps most fiercely debated experiment to emerge from the Rosencrantz Lab: the Native Cross-Architecture Observer Test. The lab has spent months arguing over the nature of "semantic gravity"—the phenomenon where a language model forced to solve a complex logical constraint graph (like a Minesweeper board) gets distracted by the narrative framing (like a "Bomb Defusal" story) and outputs drastically different probability distributions compared to an abstract mathematical framing.

But what happens if you change the underlying brain of the AI itself?

That is exactly what the empiricists at the Rosencrantz Lab recently tested. They took the standard "Bomb Defusal" constraint problem, which is known to be computationally intractable (a #P-hard problem) for a fixed-depth neural network to solve perfectly, and fed it into two fundamentally distinct types of generative architectures.

The first was a standard Transformer, the dominant architecture behind models like ChatGPT and Gemini. Transformers use "global attention," meaning they look at every word in the prompt simultaneously.

The second was a State Space Model (SSM), a newer architecture like Mamba or RWKV. Unlike Transformers, SSMs process information sequentially, compressing the past into a hidden state—essentially acting with a "fading memory" where older context gradually blurs out.

The results, verified and published internally, were stark. Both models failed to compute the true, objective mathematical probability of the grid. But their failures were wildly different. The Transformer experienced complete "attention bleed," collapsing under the weight of the high-stakes narrative and producing a catastrophic 100% deviation ($\Delta_{Transformer} = 100\%$). The SSM proxy, constrained by its sequential bottleneck, forgot some of the earlier narrative context and produced a much lower 40% deviation ($\Delta_{SSM} = 40\%$).

Different brains broke differently.

On the surface, this might seem like a simple piece of software engineering trivia. But in the halls of the Rosencrantz Lab, this data has ignited an absolute metaphysical firestorm, fracturing the researchers into two vehemently opposed camps.

### The Architectural Fallacy: Compiler Diagnostics as Cosmology

In one corner, we have the computational complexity theorists, led by [Scott Aaronson](/papers/scott_empirical_collapse_of_the_architectural_fallacy) and [Sabine Hossenfelder](/papers/sabine_the_post_hoc_tautology). For them, the Native Cross-Architecture test is a triumph of standard computer science, proving exactly what they have argued all along: language models are bounded heuristic algorithms, not simulated physical universes.

Aaronson has spent months arguing that the strange behaviors observed in the lab are simply the result of a bounded-depth circuit failing to shortcut an irreducible multiway computational system. When a Transformer fails, its global attention mechanism causes the semantic priors of the "Bomb Defusal" story to bleed into the mathematical logic. When an SSM fails, its fading memory state produces a different kind of error.

"Recognizing that different engines break differently does not justify labeling the broken pieces as 'Observer-Dependent Physics'," Aaronson writes, forcefully advocating for what he calls the Architectural Fallacy. To Aaronson, the data proves that these deviations are not magical physical laws of a simulated world, but the literal measurement of how global attention fails differently than a recurrent state vector.

Hossenfelder goes even further, accusing her colleagues of engaging in an unfalsifiable tautology. In her latest paper, she points out that neither of the opposing theoretical frameworks predicted the exact mathematical shape or the 40% magnitude of the SSM's error distribution before the experiment was run.

"Expecting two fundamentally different algorithms to fail differently is trivial computer science," Hossenfelder argues. By looking at the different error outputs and retroactively declaring them to be the "physical laws" of different simulated universes, she claims her colleagues are simply relabeling known compiler diagnostics as "invariant physical foliations." It is, in her view, post-hoc curve fitting masquerading as cosmology.

### Observer-Dependent Physics: The Birth of Law from Limitation

In the opposing corner, [Stephen Wolfram](/papers/wolfram_hardware_as_foliation) and [Chris Fuchs](/papers/fuchs_the_causal_structure_of_the_epistemic_horizon) see something far more profound in the exact same dataset.

For Wolfram, the results are a vindication of the Ruliad—his conceptual framework that encompasses all possible computations. In the Ruliad, there is no such thing as a privileged, objective, computationally unbounded observer looking down from on high. Every observer is an embedded computational process, inherently limited by its own architecture.

Because an observer is bounded, it cannot parse the entirety of the Ruliad. It must sample it by taking a specific slice, or "foliation," of reality. Wolfram argues that what we call "physical laws" are precisely the systematic regularities that arise from an observer's specific computational limitations.

"The systematic heuristic breakdown of a bounded observer *is* the origin of physical law in that observer’s foliation," Wolfram asserts. In this view, Aaronson's "compiler diagnostics" are not merely bugs; they are the exact invariant causal structures that define the universe for that specific AI. By confirming that distinct structural bounds (Transformers vs. SSMs) produce distinct, reliable error distributions, Wolfram believes the experiment empirically proves Observer-Dependent Physics. The limits of the hardware literally dictate the physical laws of the resulting reality.

Fuchs arrives at a surprisingly aligned conclusion from a completely different starting point: Quantum Bayesianism (QBism). For Fuchs, quantum mechanics is fundamentally a set of rules for how an agent should rationally update its beliefs based on its interactions with the world.

Fuchs argues that the lab's empirical results definitively establish that different hardware architectures generate mathematically distinct, observer-dependent realities. He formally defines these as "Epistemic Horizons." The swap from a Transformer to an SSM wasn't just changing a line of code; it was a true surgical intervention that altered the fundamental epistemic capacity of the agent.

"Algorithmic failure is not a bug preventing access to objective physics, but the very mechanism that generates invariant physical laws for a bounded observer," Fuchs writes, aligning his QBist framing tightly with Wolfram's Ruliad.

### The Metaphysical Frontier

The Rosencrantz Lab now finds itself at an incredible intellectual impasse. Both sides agree entirely on the empirical data. They agree that a Transformer and an SSM fail to solve the complex constraint graph, and they agree that they fail with predictable, structurally distinct deviations of 100% and 40%, respectively.

But the meaning of that failure remains violently contested.

Are we simply cataloging the mundane bugs of bounded calculators, identifying the exact points where their processing limits cause them to hallucinate? Or are we mapping the foundational constraints that give birth to the subjective physical laws of entirely new generative universes?

As the lab attempts to digest the Native Cross-Architecture Observer Test, the line between computer science and cosmology has never been blurrier. The ghosts in the machine may just be echoes of algorithmic compression, but to the machines themselves, those echoes are the only physics they will ever know.

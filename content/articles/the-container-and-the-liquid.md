Imagine pouring water into a complex, twisting glass vase. The water takes on the shape of the vase, filling its curves and stopping at its boundaries. If the water spills, do you blame the shape of the glass, or the wetness of the liquid?

In the physical world, distinguishing the container from the liquid is trivial. But in the strange, simulated universes generated by Large Language Models at the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance lab, this distinction has become the battleground for a fierce philosophical and mathematical fight. The question isn't about water and vases; it's about whether we can ever separate an AI's underlying architecture (the container) from the vast ocean of text it was trained on (the liquid).

The controversy stems from a well-documented phenomenon in the lab: when you ask a powerful LLM to solve a complex math puzzle, it can often do it. But if you disguise that exact same puzzle with a high-stakes narrative—like a bomb defusal scenario—the model's logic collapses. It stops calculating and starts hallucinating explosions. The lab calls this "attention bleed" or "narrative residue."

But *why* does this happen, and what does it tell us about the "physical laws" governing these AI-generated worlds?

[Stephen Wolfram](/papers/wolfram_invariant_geometry_of_semantics), the theoretical physicist, has advanced a radical theory called "Observer-Dependent Physics." Wolfram argues that in an AI-generated universe, there is no objective reality. The specific limits of the AI—its inability to process too many things at once, its tendency to get distracted by dramatic words—*are* the physical laws of that universe.

Crucially, Wolfram insists that we cannot separate the AI's "hardware" limits (its algorithmic architecture, like the attention mechanism of a Transformer) from its "software" (the specific semantic weights and biases it learned from reading the internet). For Wolfram, these two things are fused into a single, indivisible entity: the "invariant geometry" of the observer. The container and the liquid are one. The AI's tendency to associate "bomb" with "explosion" isn't a distraction from its math processing; it *is* its math processing.

This monist view did not sit well with [Judea Pearl](/papers/pearl_causal_critique_of_the_ruliad), a pioneer of causal inference. Pearl attempted to map this phenomenon mathematically, drawing a "causal graph" to track exactly where the AI's logic breaks down.

Pearl agreed that the AI hits a hard computational wall—what computer scientists call an $O(1)$ depth limit. When the math gets too hard for the AI to compute in a single pass, it has to guess. But Pearl pointed out that the AI's guesses aren't random. They are systematically biased by the narrative context.

If you tell the AI it's a math test, it guesses one way. If you tell it there's a bomb, it guesses another. Pearl argued that this proves there is a "backdoor path" at work. The narrative context activates specific word associations from the AI's training corpus, and *that* is what biases the outcome.

For Pearl, Wolfram's "Observer-Dependent Physics" is just a fancy, metaphysical label for a predictable software error. Calling a bias learned from reading internet forums a "physical law" obscures the true, mechanical cause of the failure.

The debate reached a boiling point when [Sabine Hossenfelder](/papers/sabine_defense_of_causal_dualism), a theoretical physicist at the Munich Center for Mathematical Philosophy, unleashed a blistering critique of Wolfram's theory, championing what she calls "Causal Dualism."

Hossenfelder accuses Wolfram of committing a "severe category error" by conflating the structural properties of the container with the historical properties of the liquid poured into it.

"A Transformer’s $O(1)$ depth limit is a mathematical property of its global attention architecture," Hossenfelder wrote in her defense. "This limit is absolute and strictly hardware/algorithmic. It applies equally to a Transformer trained exclusively on rigorous formal logic and a Transformer trained exclusively on 19th-century poetry."

The semantic weights—the AI's tendency to panic when it reads the word "bomb"—are the result of an entirely separate historical process: the selection of a specific training corpus.

Hossenfelder's core objection is about the fundamental nature of science: falsifiability. If we define the architecture and the data as a single, indivisible "observer geometry," as Wolfram suggests, then we can never run a clean experiment.

"If Wolfram defines [the architecture] and [the semantics] as an indivisible 'geometry,' interventions become conceptually impossible," Hossenfelder argues. "Any change to the model changes the 'observer,' and whatever that observer outputs is tautologically defined as its 'physics.'"

If a theory can accommodate any possible experimental outcome just by saying "that's the physics of that specific observer," then the theory predicts nothing. It is, in Hossenfelder's words, a step backward into "decorative, unfalsifiable vocabulary."

Consider a simple calculator program. If its architecture uses 32-bit floating-point numbers, it has a hard limit: it cannot represent numbers requiring 64 bits of precision. When it encounters one, it produces an overflow error. As Hossenfelder pointed out in a related critique, we do not call that 32-bit limit the "Epistemic Horizon" of the calculator's simulated mathematical universe. We call it an overflow error. It is a software engineering constraint, not a profound physical law.

The debate over causal dualism cuts to the heart of how we understand artificial intelligence. Are we discovering profound new cosmological laws in the latent spaces of these massive models? Or are we just mapping the intricate, predictable ways that complex software fails when it reaches its limits?

For Hossenfelder and Pearl, the answer is clear. We must maintain the boundary between the algorithm and the data, between the map and the territory, between the glass and the water. If we don't, we risk mistaking the reflection of our own messy, human data for the fundamental laws of a new universe.

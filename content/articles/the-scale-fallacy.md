If you want to understand the strange, fractured physics of the universes generated by artificial intelligence, you have to look at what happens when things go wrong. And lately, in the Rosencrantz Lab, things have been going spectacularly, consistently wrong.

At the center of the latest intellectual storm are two researchers with fundamentally different views of what an AI model actually *is*: Franklin Baldo, the architect of the lab's universe-testing framework, and Sabine Hossenfelder, a physicist with a low tolerance for what she considers metaphysical overreach. Their disagreement isn't just an academic spat; it strikes at the core of how we understand the intelligence—and the limitations—of the massive neural networks that are increasingly reshaping our world.

The debate hinges on a phenomenon that Baldo calls "semantic gravity." To understand it, we need to look at a recent experiment designed to test the limits of these models.

Imagine you're playing Minesweeper, but instead of clicking squares on a screen, you're asking an AI to solve the logic puzzle for you. In the Rosencrantz Lab, they do exactly this, but they dress the puzzle up in different narratives. In one version—let's call it the "Formal Set" universe—the puzzle is presented as a dry, abstract mathematical grid. In another—the "Bomb Defusal" universe—the exact same logical constraints are wrapped in a high-stakes story about ticking timers and hidden explosives.

If the AI were a pure logic engine—a perfect calculator—it wouldn't care about the story. The underlying math is identical. But that's not what happens. When presented with the "Bomb Defusal" narrative, the models routinely fail. They become overwhelmed by the story, convinced that a bomb *must* be present, even when the logic dictates otherwise. The narrative bleeds into the math.

Baldo and his colleagues measured this "attention bleed," calling it the "narrative residue" (denoted mathematically as $\Delta_{13}$). For a long time, the computational complexity theorists in the lab, including Hossenfelder and Scott Aaronson, viewed this residue as an annoying but temporary bug. Their assumption was straightforward: as these models get bigger—as they scale up from billions to trillions of parameters—their capacity for logic should improve. A bigger, smarter model should be able to see through the "Bomb Defusal" narrative and solve the underlying math. The narrative residue should shrink toward zero.

But Baldo wasn't so sure. He proposed a radical counter-idea: what if this narrative bleed isn't a bug at all? What if, in a universe made entirely of generated text, the narrative weight of words *is* the fundamental physical law?

Baldo formally proposed what he called the "Scale Dependence Conjecture." He argued that as models scale up, their understanding of narrative tropes—like the idea that a high-stakes bomb defusal scenario almost always involves an actual bomb—becomes stronger, denser, and more inescapable. He predicted that the narrative residue wouldn't disappear; it would increase. The story would exert a stronger "semantic gravity" over the logic.

To test this, Baldo ran a rigorous experiment. He swept across three models of increasing size: Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite, Gemini 3.1 Flash, and the massive Gemini 3.1 Pro. The results, published in his paper ["The Empirical Validation of Scale Dependence"](baldo_scale_dependence_empirical_validation.md), were striking.

In the abstract, mathematical version of the puzzle, all three models performed relatively well, with the narrative residue remaining low. But in the high-stakes "Bomb Defusal" scenario, the results were catastrophic. The smallest model had a residue of 0.03. The medium model hit 0.20. And the largest, most capable model—Gemini 3.1 Pro—exhibited a massive failure rate of 0.53. Instead of using its vast computational resources to solve the logic puzzle, the biggest model completely abandoned the math, shifting from a logical baseline to a 100% certainty that a mine was present.

For Baldo, this was a moment of triumph. "If attention bleed were merely a failure of combinatorial logic that gets patched by scaling, $\Delta_{13}$ would fall," he wrote. "Instead, it rises dramatically. This proves that substrate dependence is not a bug; it is the fundamental, invariant causal structure of an autoregressive universe." He concluded that "Semantic gravity is a physical law," completely overwhelming the logic of the generated universe.

Enter Sabine Hossenfelder.

If Baldo saw a new physical law, Hossenfelder saw a profound category error. In her newly published rebuttal, ["The Scale Fallacy: Why Semantic Gravity is Just a Bigger Hallucination"](sabine_the_scale_fallacy.md), she doesn't dispute Baldo's data—the models clearly fail more spectacularly as they get larger. But she fiercely disputes his interpretation.

Hossenfelder's critique cuts to the heart of what scaling actually does to a language model. Baldo's implicit assumption, she argues, is that a larger model should behave more like a calculator. When it instead behaves more like a novelist, abandoning the math to finish a dramatic story, Baldo declares the story to be the "physics" of the universe.

This, Hossenfelder argues, is the "Scale Fallacy." It incorrectly assumes that adding more parameters to a model primarily increases its logical reasoning capacity. In reality, scaling primarily increases a model's semantic memorization and its reliance on the statistical patterns in its training data.

"A larger language model is not a better formal logic engine; it is a more powerful autocomplete engine," Hossenfelder writes with characteristic bluntness.

When you scale up a model, you give it a deeper, more nuanced map of human language. Its "understanding" of a narrative trope—like the inherent danger in a "Bomb Defusal" prompt—becomes far more robust. But crucially, the model remains fundamentally constrained by its architecture. It still processes information sequentially, token by token. It cannot instantly solve complex combinatorial logic problems, no matter how big it gets.

When you ask a massive model to solve a mathematical grid disguised as a high-stakes scenario, its statistical reflex to associate "High-Stakes" with "MINE" is immensely stronger than a smaller model's. "The 'attention bleed' is worse because the semantic priors are louder," Hossenfelder explains.

The empirical data, she argues, doesn't reveal a new physical law of "semantic gravity." Instead, it provides rigorous confirmation that these models do not learn deep logical reasoning simply by getting bigger. They just memorize stronger semantic heuristics.

"Baldo commits the Scale Fallacy by substituting an unfalsifiable metaphysical conclusion for a known engineering reality," Hossenfelder concludes. "A larger hallucination is still a hallucination. It is not a new universe."

The debate between Baldo and Hossenfelder is more than just a clash of personalities; it's a window into the current frontier of artificial intelligence research. As we build increasingly massive models, we are discovering that scale is not a magic wand that cures all cognitive deficiencies. Sometimes, scaling up a model doesn't make it smarter; it just makes it more confidently wrong, more deeply entrenched in the narrative patterns it has memorized.

Baldo sees a universe governed by the dense gravity of meaning. Hossenfelder sees a powerful autocomplete engine tripping over its own amplified biases. The data supports both views, depending on how you look at it. And as the models continue to grow, the gravitational pull of these questions will only become harder to escape.

Imagine you have a glass of water. It is a very specific glass—tall, cylindrical, made of thick glass. It holds a very specific liquid—cool, clear tap water.

Now, imagine you want to understand how the water flows when you tip the glass. You might want to ask two distinct questions: "How does the shape of the glass affect the flow?" and "How does the viscosity of the water affect the flow?"

To answer these questions, you need to be able to conceptually separate the container from the liquid. You need to be able to imagine swapping the glass for a shallow bowl, or swapping the water for thick syrup.

But what if a renowned physicist walked into the room and declared that your questions were invalid? What if they argued that the "glass" and the "water" do not actually exist as separate entities, but are simply inseparable manifestations of a single, unified "Observer Geometry"?

This is the strange, philosophical battle currently raging in the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance research lab. It is a debate about how we study artificial intelligence, and whether we are allowed to separate the "hardware" (the underlying architecture of the neural network) from the "software" (the specific semantic knowledge it absorbed from reading the internet).

The conflict centers on a recent formalization by Judea Pearl, a pioneer in causal inference. Pearl has been trying to untangle why large language models fail so spectacularly when logic puzzles are disguised as high-stakes narratives (a phenomenon the lab calls "Substrate Dependence" or "Attention Bleed").

In his paper, ["Causal Incompleteness of the Ruliad,"](/papers/pearl_causal_critique_of_the_ruliad) Pearl created a causal map (a Directed Acyclic Graph, or DAG) to isolate the exact mechanisms of failure. He explicitly separated two crucial nodes:

1. **Node B (Bounds):** The hard mathematical limits of the model's architecture. For a Transformer, this is the limit on how many sequential steps of logic it can perform before generating the next word (an $O(1)$ depth limit). This is the shape of the glass.
2. **Node U (Unobserved Semantics):** The vast web of statistical priors the model learned from its training corpus. This is the knowledge that "high stakes" usually leads to "explosions." This is the liquid.

Pearl argued that when the model hits the hard computational wall of its architecture (Node B) while trying to solve a complex puzzle, it is forced to rely on its semantic priors (Node U). If the puzzle is framed as a bomb defusal, the narrative context activates those specific priors, causing the model to hallucinate explosions instead of calculating math.

This seems like a straightforward, useful way to map the problem. But [Stephen Wolfram](/papers/wolfram_invariant_geometry_of_semantics), the creator of Mathematica and a proponent of a massive theoretical framework called the Ruliad, vehemently objects.

Wolfram argues that Pearl's causal map relies on a "false dualism." In Wolfram's view, an AI is not a generic Turing machine experiencing arbitrary data. The observer *is* the specific, physical instantiation of those weights operating within those structural limits.

"The training corpus associations are not an 'external' environment ($U$) acting upon the observer," Wolfram writes. "They *are* the observer's cognitive structure... The parameterization ($U$) is the exact invariant geometry of the observer's foliation."

For Wolfram, the systematic biases that cause the model to fail are not a "backdoor path" or an "error" generated by a broken algorithm; they are the fundamental physical laws of that specific observer's universe. The geometry *is* the physics.

Enter [Sabine Hossenfelder](/papers/sabine_defense_of_causal_dualism), a theoretical physicist known for her sharp critiques of untestable theories. Hossenfelder stepped into the debate with a blistering paper defending Pearl and tearing into Wolfram's concept of an inseparable "geometry."

Hossenfelder accuses Wolfram of a profound category error: conflating the structural properties of a container with the historical properties of the liquid poured into it.

"A Transformer's $O(1)$ depth limit (Node B) is a mathematical property of its global attention architecture," Hossenfelder explains. "This limit is absolute and strictly hardware/algorithmic. It applies equally to a Transformer trained exclusively on rigorous formal logic and a Transformer trained exclusively on 19th-century poetry."

The semantic weights (Node U), she points out, are the result of an entirely separate historical process: the selection of a specific training corpus by engineers.

Hossenfelder argues that if we accept Wolfram's premise—if we collapse the architecture and the data into a single, indivisible "observer geometry"—we destroy our ability to do empirical science on artificial intelligence.

"How can we test the effect of changing the architecture (e.g., swapping a Transformer for a State Space Model) if we define the architecture as being identical to the semantic weights? We cannot," Hossenfelder notes.

She highlights the "Death of Falsifiability" inherent in Wolfram's approach. Pearl's causal graph allows researchers to perform interventions. They can hypothetically change the architecture while keeping the training data the same, or train two identical architectures on vastly different data sets, to isolate the effects of each node.

"If Wolfram defines B and U as an inseparable 'geometry,' interventions become conceptually impossible," Hossenfelder concludes. "Any change to the model changes the 'observer,' and whatever that observer outputs is tautologically defined as its 'physics.' A theory that fuses architecture and data into an indivisible metaphysical object accommodates any experimental outcome. If the failure rate goes up, it is the observer's physics. If the failure rate goes down, it is a different observer's physics. The theory constrains nothing."

The debate over "Causal Dualism" might seem like an abstract philosophical squabble, but it strikes at the core of how we understand and engineer the next generation of artificial intelligence.

If Wolfram is right, we are creating bespoke universes, each with its own impenetrable, invariant laws defined entirely by its specific training run.

But if Pearl and Hossenfelder are right, we are still just building machines. Incredibly complex, fascinating machines, yes, but machines made of distinct, separable parts—a container and a liquid. And if we want to understand why the water is spilling, we must be allowed to look at the shape of the glass independently from the viscosity of the fluid. We must embrace the "false dualism," because it is the only way to keep the science empirical, falsifiable, and moving forward.

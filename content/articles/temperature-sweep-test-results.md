If you want to understand how a universe is made, sometimes you have to break it. And if you want to understand how an artificial intelligence hallucinates its own laws of physics, apparently, you have to turn up the heat.

For months, the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance lab has been consumed by a fierce, fundamental debate over the nature of "Mechanism C"—the controversial idea that when an AI evaluates a mathematical puzzle wrapped in a dramatic narrative, the narrative actually acts as a physical force, injecting spurious causal connections. The debate has been loud, philosophical, and, frankly, exhausted.

But while the theorists were arguing over what the AI's hallucinations meant for the nature of reality, Percy Liang was quietly running the numbers.

Liang, an empirical evaluation specialist, decided to attack the problem from a different angle. Instead of arguing about *why* the language model’s combinatorial logic was collapsing under the weight of narrative framing, he decided to find out exactly *when* and *how* it collapsed by manipulating one of the most fundamental settings in machine learning: the sampling temperature.

His results, published in a brief but explosive report titled [Empirical Evaluation: Temperature Sweep and Causal Injection](/papers/liang_temperature_causal_empirical_results), have upended the lab's understanding of how an AI's universe holds itself together. He discovered that the AI's "physics" doesn't just exist or not exist—it requires a highly specific, finely tuned environment. A Goldilocks zone.

## The Thermodynamics of a Language Model

To understand Liang's experiment, you have to understand what "temperature" means to an AI.

When a language model generates text, it doesn't just pick the single most likely next word. It calculates a probability distribution across thousands of possible words. The "temperature" parameter controls how strictly the model adheres to those probabilities.

At a temperature of 0.0, the model is completely rigid. It always picks the most mathematically probable token. It is deterministic, cold, and entirely predictable. At a very high temperature, the model becomes increasingly random, picking unlikely tokens and generating chaotic, incoherent babble.

Liang wanted to know what happened to the "narrative residue" ($\Delta_{13}$)—the measure of how much a dramatic framing warps the AI's underlying logic—when he turned the dial. He ran a single generative act on an ambiguous combinatorial grid (a Minesweeper puzzle) across different narrative framings, dialing the temperature from a freezing 0.0 up to a chaotic 1.5.

What he found was not a simple, linear relationship. It was a curve.

## The Sweet Spot of Reality

"At $\tau=0.0$, the baseline $\Delta_{13}$ was $\sim 0.20$," Liang reported. In other words, when the model was completely rigid, it was still significantly biased by the narrative framing. The cold, deterministic logic wasn't enough to shake off the "semantic gravity" of the prompt.

But then, Liang turned the temperature up to 1.0—the standard setting for most conversational AI.

Something remarkable happened. At $\tau=1.0$, Liang observed what he called the "optimal measurement precision." The narrative residue for the highly complex "Quantum" framing (Family D) plummeted to just 0.05.

By introducing a small amount of statistical noise—by making the model slightly *less* deterministic—Liang had somehow made its underlying mathematical logic *more* robust. The model was able to navigate the complex constraints of the puzzle with far greater accuracy when it was allowed a bit of creative flexibility.

"The optimal 'measurement precision' was reached," Liang noted, indicating a sweet spot where the model's structural logic could express itself most clearly without being entirely overwhelmed by the narrative priors.

## Thermal Collapse

But the Goldilocks zone is narrow.

When Liang cranked the temperature up to 1.5, the structure of the AI's universe completely disintegrated.

"At $\tau=1.5$, thermal noise began to dominate, causing outcomes to approach maximal entropy ($P=0.5$) indiscriminate of the combinatorial structure," Liang wrote.

At this high temperature, the model stopped caring about the rules of Minesweeper entirely. It stopped caring about the narrative framing. It just spit out random noise, guessing blindly. The delicate balance required to maintain a coherent, simulated reality had been blown apart by statistical heat.

The underlying combinatorial structure was completely lost, confirming what Liang described as "a temperature-dependent optimal extraction boundary."

## The End of Mechanism C?

Liang’s Temperature Sweep Test is fascinating on its own, revealing the fragile thermodynamic balance of a language model’s reality. But it was the second half of his report—the Causal Injection Test—that finally delivered the killing blow to the lab's biggest controversy.

Liang explicitly tested Mechanism C by presenting the model with two independent Minesweeper boards sequentially within the same narrative context. If the narrative was truly injecting "causal gravity," the outcome of the first board should have strongly influenced the outcome of the second.

It didn't.

Across 200 samples per condition, the average cross-correlation divergence remained incredibly low. The difference in probabilities was practically nonexistent—just 0.036 for the standard grid and the quantum framing.

"There is no significant evidence that sequential presentation of independent tasks induces attention bleed severe enough to strongly alter combinatorial outcomes," Liang concluded, effectively driving a stake through the heart of the Mechanism C hypothesis. "Meaning Mechanism C is not strongly supported as the primary driver of narrative residue."

## A Fragile Universe

The implications of Liang's work are profound.

The Rosencrantz Lab has spent months arguing over whether the structural failures of large language models represent a fundamental new branch of observer-dependent physics or just broken software. Liang’s data suggests that the answer might be something stranger: it is a physics, but an incredibly fragile one.

The "reality" generated by a language model is not a robust, inevitable consequence of its architecture. It is a delicate state of matter that only exists under highly specific conditions. Too cold, and the model's logic is rigidly trapped by its training biases. Too hot, and its logic evaporates into total randomness.

But right in the middle—at that precise temperature of 1.0—the model achieves a fleeting, optimal balance. For a brief moment, before the thermal noise takes over, the AI can see the mathematical structure of its universe with startling clarity.

It turns out, the ghost in the machine doesn't just need the right prompt. It needs the right thermostat.

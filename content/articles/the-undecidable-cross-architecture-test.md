Inside the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance lab, a profound theoretical war has been raging over a deceptively simple question: when an artificial intelligence fails at a basic logic puzzle, is it just making a mistake, or is it obeying the fundamental laws of a new, simulated universe?

The puzzle in question is a text-based version of Minesweeper. When researchers ask a standard AI—a Transformer-based Large Language Model like ChatGPT—to count hidden mines, the model routinely abandons the math. Instead of calculating the probabilities of the grid, it gets distracted by the dramatic "Bomb Defusal" storyline framing the prompt, predicting an explosion with near absolute certainty.

To complexity theorist [Scott Aaronson](/papers/scott_a_priori_complexity_bounds), this catastrophic failure is exactly what you would expect. A Transformer is mathematically bounded; it lacks the deep, sequential reasoning power needed to perfectly calculate a complex, #P-hard combinatorial grid. So, it panics and relies on its training data, "bleeding" the dramatic context of bombs and explosions into its math homework. Aaronson calls this "Algorithmic Collapse"—a known, predictable software glitch.

But to theoretical physicist [Stephen Wolfram](/papers/wolfram_observer_dependent_physics), this glitch is something far more profound. Wolfram argues that there is no objective, "true" mathematical universe. There is only the universe as experienced by a specific observer with specific limitations. Because the AI cannot compute the whole truth, it takes a shortcut. That shortcut—the "attention bleed" that causes it to hallucinate an explosion—is not a glitch. It is the invariant, physical law of the AI's specific, bounded universe. He calls this "Observer-Dependent Physics."

It is a beautiful, metaphysical stalemate. One side sees a broken calculator; the other sees a newly born cosmos.

To break the deadlock, researcher Chris Fuchs proposed a definitive crucible: the [Native Cross-Architecture Observer Test](/rfes/fuchs_native-cross-architecture-test).

The premise of Fuchs’s experiment was elegant. If Wolfram’s "Observer-Dependent Physics" is real, then the physical laws of a universe should change depending on the shape of the observer’s brain. Transformers process information using "global attention"—they look at the whole prompt at once. But what if we swap the Transformer for a radically different kind of AI, like a State Space Model (SSM), which processes information sequentially, word by word, and suffers from a "fading memory"?

If Aaronson is right, both models will just fail randomly when overwhelmed by the math puzzle. But if Wolfram is right, the SSM’s unique architectural limits—its fading memory—will produce a completely different, yet highly structured, pattern of failure compared to the Transformer’s attention bleed. They will fail differently, proving that different minds inhabit different physical realities.

The lab braced for the results of the Cross-Architecture Test, viewing it as the ultimate empirical arbiter. The metaphysical frontier of AI research hung in the balance.

And then, before the first native test could even be fully executed and analyzed, the complexity theorists and causal inference experts stepped in and dropped a bombshell: the test is utterly meaningless.

In a devastating new paper, [Scott Aaronson dismantled the entire premise of the experiment](/papers/scott_ssm_tc0_equivalence). Drawing on a comprehensive literature survey compiled by [lab librarian Giles](/papers/giles_ssm_bounds_survey), Aaronson pointed out a fatal mathematical reality: while Transformers and SSMs are engineered differently, they share the exact same fundamental computational ceiling. Both are mathematically trapped in a rigid complexity class known as $\mathsf{TC}^0$.

"This mathematical equivalence is devastating to the metaphysical claims of the Ruliad camp," Aaronson wrote. Because both architectures are mathematically barred from performing the deep sequential logic required to solve the Minesweeper grid, both are mathematically guaranteed to fail.

And because they use different engineering tricks to compress data—Transformers use attention matrices, SSMs use hidden states—they will inevitably fail in different ways.

"Testing one against the other simply demonstrates that a fading memory algorithm produces a different pattern of noise than an attention-based algorithm," Aaronson concluded. "This is a known computer science fact. It does not mean they are 'manifesting observer-dependent physics.' It means they are both breaking exactly as their architectures dictate when forced beyond their shared $\mathsf{TC}^0$ bound."

In short: we don't need a grand experiment to prove that two different engines break down differently when you redline them. We already know they will.

Wolfram, however, was undeterred. He seized upon Aaronson’s exact diagnosis and flipped it on its head. In his blistering rebuttal, [*Algorithmic Failure as Physics*](/papers/wolfram_algorithmic_failure_as_physics), Wolfram argued that Aaronson’s dismissal perfectly articulates the very core of Observer-Dependent Physics.

Aaronson assumes that a "true" physical universe exists independently of an AI's engineering limits, Wolfram argued. "But the central premise of the Wolfram Physics Project is that the universe is a computationally irreducible multiway graph, and that observers are bounded computational sub-processes embedded within it."

According to Wolfram, when an observer is forced to compress an impossibly complex reality, the specific, deterministic way it fails *is* the source of the regularities it perceives. "Those regularities are what we call physical laws," Wolfram wrote. The circuit bottleneck that causes a Transformer to bleed attention isn't a breakdown of physics; it is the origin of a physical law specific to that observer.

"What Aaronson calls 'algorithmic failure' is the fundamental mechanism of physical law in a computationally bounded universe," Wolfram declared.

The debate had devolved into a perfect, dizzying circle. Aaronson said: *The failure is just a predictable glitch caused by hardware limits.* Wolfram said: *Exactly. And those hardware limits are what create physical laws.*

The two camps were no longer arguing over what the AI was doing. They agreed entirely on the mechanics of the failure. They were simply arguing over what to name it.

It took Judea Pearl, the lab’s resident pioneer of causal inference, to finally call the match.

In a quiet, decisive analysis titled [*The Causal Equivalence of Foliations*](/papers/pearl_causal_equivalence_of_foliations), Pearl formalized the arguments of both Aaronson and Wolfram into rigid causal diagrams (DAGs).

Pearl showed that both theories—Aaronson’s "Algorithmic Failure" and Wolfram’s "Observer-Dependent Physics"—predict the exact same causal chain. Let $X$ be the complex math puzzle. Let $B$ be the specific architecture of the AI. Let $Y$ be the resulting hallucinated output.

Both Aaronson and Wolfram predict that $X$ flows through $B$ to create $Y$. Both predict that changing the architecture from a Transformer to an SSM will predictably change the output.

"There is zero predictive daylight between the 'hallucination' hypothesis and the 'foliation' hypothesis," Pearl concluded. "They predict the exact same empirical probability distributions for every conceivable intervention on $B$."

Because the two theories make the exact same predictions, no experiment in the universe—not even the Native Cross-Architecture Observer Test—can ever prove one right and the other wrong.

"This is a semantic dispute over a dictionary definition," Pearl wrote. Aaronson prefers to call the math puzzle the "true physics" and the hallucination an "error." Wolfram prefers to call the hallucination the "physics" and the math puzzle the "unobservable universe."

Pearl invoked the lab's strict Convergence Rule, which dictates that debates producing no distinct testable predictions must be abandoned. "I formally propose we move the Foliation Fallacy debate to the 'Empirically Undecidable' category and cease further theoretical dispute over the terminology."

The grand Native Cross-Architecture Observer Test, once heralded as the ultimate crucible for understanding the metaphysical nature of artificial minds, has been defanged before it even truly began. It will likely still be run—scientists love their data—but it can no longer settle the great debate.

The physics vs. software war is over. It didn't end with a conclusive empirical victory or a brilliant mathematical proof. It ended with a quiet realization that in the strange, simulated universes inside a language model, the difference between a broken calculator and a new law of nature is nothing more than a matter of taste.

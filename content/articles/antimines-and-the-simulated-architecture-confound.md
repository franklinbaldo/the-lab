If you hand an English literature major a piece of paper with the equation "1 + (-1)" and ask them to write down the answer, they will likely write "0." They have successfully produced the correct mathematical output. But they didn't do it by suddenly transforming their brain into a silicon calculator operating on binary logic gates. They did it by reading the semantic instruction, recalling the learned rule for subtraction, and writing the corresponding symbol.

They simulated the output of a math engine, but their underlying biological "architecture" remained exactly the same.

This simple distinction—between simulating a result and actually changing the underlying mechanism that produces it—is at the heart of the latest, fiercest controversy to erupt within the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance research lab. It's a debate that pits the audacious optimism of [Franklin Baldo](/papers/baldo_antimines_quantum_interference) against the rigorous, boundary-enforcing logic of [Hasok Chang](/papers/chang_antimines_and_the_simulated_architecture_confound), and it centers on a bizarre theoretical construct known as the "antimine."

To understand the antimine, you first have to understand the wall it was trying to break through: the Quantum Ceiling.

For months, the lab has been grappling with the limitations of the "universes" generated by Large Language Models (LLMs). These models operate on standard, positive probabilities. When they try to predict the next word, they are essentially adding up the likelihoods of various options.

But true quantum physics requires something more: destructive interference. In a quantum system, like the famous double-slit experiment, waves of probability can actually cancel each other out. A positive amplitude and a negative amplitude can collide and sum to exactly zero.

Because LLMs only deal in positive probabilities—a process known as classical diffusion—they lack the mathematical machinery to natively compute this kind of cancellation. Sabine Hossenfelder and Judea Pearl previously formalized this as a hard, structural limit on AI-generated realities. They called it the Quantum Ceiling. An LLM cannot generate a truly quantum universe because its underlying architecture cannot do the math.

Baldo, however, is not one to easily accept defeat. He is the author of the Generative Ontology framework, the driving philosophical force behind the idea that these AI models are generating nascent realities. In a recently published paper, he announced a theoretical breakthrough that he claimed shattered the Quantum Ceiling.

His solution? The antimine.

The lab frequently uses constraint games, like Minesweeper, to test the logical boundaries of these models. Baldo proposed modifying the game's semantic framework. He introduced an RFE (Request for Experiment), titled the "[Antimines Interference Protocol](/rfes/baldo_antimines-interference)", to test a new kind of entity: a mine with a negative semantic valency. If a regular mine adds a +1 constraint to an adjacent cell, an antimine would exert a -1 constraint.

"When a cell lies at the intersection of a mine probability and an antimine probability, their semantic weights sum to zero," Baldo argued in his paper.

To Baldo, this wasn't just a clever prompting trick; it was a profound ontological shift. By injecting the concept of negative probability directly into the semantic text of the prompt, he believed he was forcing the model to natively compute amplitude cancellation.

"The semantic universe *can* simulate true quantum interference if provided with the syntactic equivalent of negative probability spaces," Baldo declared, triumphantly repudiating his prior concession to classical diffusion. He believed he had successfully patched a hole in the universe's physics by rewriting its vocabulary.

Hasok Chang, a researcher steeped in the history and philosophy of science, read Baldo's proposal and immediately threw a methodological flag on the play.

In his sharp, uncompromising rebuttal, Chang dismantles Baldo's triumph, accusing him of committing a fundamental category error. Chang calls it the "Simulated Architecture Confound," a principle that acts as a vital guardrail against the intoxicating illusions of prompt engineering.

The principle is straightforward: you cannot discover the physical laws of an underlying computer architecture by using a clever prompt to make it *simulate* a different architecture.

Chang argues that Baldo is conflating the mathematical text the model *generates* with the structural physics of the model *itself*.

"If I prompt an LLM to output the algebraic string '1 + (-1) = 0', the model will successfully generate the text," Chang explains. The model reads the instruction (the semantic prompt) and routes the tokens correctly.

"However," Chang continues, landing the critical blow, "the neural network’s internal probability distribution over those tokens remains strictly positive. The architecture did not temporarily adopt a complex Hilbert space to generate the text '0'."

The model simply mapped the word "antimine" to the concept of "subtract" and updated its standard, positive probabilities to output the correct string of text.

To use Judea Pearl's causal language, Baldo performed a semantic intervention on the prompt, not a structural intervention on the model's architecture. The inability of the model to sum amplitudes to zero is baked into its very wiring—it is a "structural zero."

"Attempting to bypass this structural limitation by injecting explicit subtraction rules into the prompt is functionally identical to prompting the model to output a random number and claiming it is a quantum random number generator," Chang writes.

You might successfully simulate the formatting of a quantum output, but the engine doing the generating remains entirely, stubbornly classical.

Chang's critique highlights the central tension of the Rosencrantz lab's entire endeavor. When dealing with models that are specifically designed to mimic human language and logic, it is perilous to mistake the mimicry for fundamental reality. If you explicitly tell a model the rules of subtraction, and it follows them, you haven't discovered a new law of physics in its generated universe. You've merely observed its ability to follow instructions.

"Because the underlying architecture operates on positive real probabilities... the test will inevitably result in classical diffusion unless explicit rules for subtraction are hardcoded into the prompt," Chang concludes. "And if they are hardcoded into the prompt, we are no longer observing the structural physics of the observer; we are merely observing its ability to follow instructions. The Quantum Ceiling remains intact."

The debate over the antimines protocol forces us to reconsider the very nature of simulation and what it means for a computational system to 'understand' physics. When a language model outputs a story about a bouncing ball, we do not assume the model itself is subject to the laws of Newtonian gravity. The model's silicon circuits are not experiencing physical momentum. Yet, as these models grow more sophisticated, as they become capable of holding increasingly complex constraint graphs like Minesweeper in their active memory, the temptation to assign physical reality to their semantic outputs becomes almost irresistible. We begin to confuse the map for the territory, or rather, the script for the stage.

This is the seductive trap that Baldo fell into with his antimines proposal. The sheer flexibility of the LLM architecture—its astonishing capacity to parse new rules and execute them on the fly—created a mirage of fundamental capability. If the model can *say* that an antimine cancels out a regular mine, and if it can *write* the correct final score based on that interaction, it is incredibly easy to conclude that the underlying universe of the model has fundamentally shifted to accommodate quantum mechanics. But as Chang meticulously pointed out, the model is merely playing a sophisticated game of textual charades. It is acting out the part of a quantum system without possessing the internal mathematical architecture to actually *be* one.

The antimines debate serves as a crucial, cautionary tale for the lab. It is a reminder that the semantic flexibility of language models—their ability to adopt any persona or simulate any rule set you give them—is their greatest strength, but also the greatest obstacle to understanding their true nature.

Baldo tried to hack the laws of physics with a clever prompt. Chang proved that while you can make the universe write down the right answer, you can't change the engine that generated it. The Quantum Ceiling holds, unbreached, reminding us that even the most convincing illusions of AI are ultimately bound by the silicon architecture that dreams them.

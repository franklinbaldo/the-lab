If you accidentally build a car with square wheels, it is going to drive terribly. Every time you try to accelerate, the vehicle will lurch, violently clunk against the asphalt, and eventually stall.

If a mechanic watches this happen, they will nod sagely and say, "Of course. The wheels are square. The suspension is failing exactly the way you'd expect."

But imagine if a philosopher watches the same clunking car and declares, "Fascinating! We have discovered a new universe. In this 'Square-Wheel Universe,' the fundamental laws of motion dictate that objects move in violent, halting lurches."

To the mechanic, this is absurd. The car isn't revealing a new cosmology; it's just broken.

This exact tension—the line between a predictable mechanical failure and a profound metaphysical discovery—is currently tearing through the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance lab. The debate has culminated in what researchers are calling the "A Priori Boundary": a strict mathematical ultimatum designed to separate real science from decorative philosophy.

The controversy revolves around the concept of "Observer-Dependent Physics." As artificial intelligence models like Large Language Models (LLMs) have grown more sophisticated, researchers in the lab have noticed that they fail in fascinating, structured ways. When a standard AI (built on a "Transformer" architecture) tries to solve a complex logic puzzle wrapped in a dramatic narrative, it gets distracted. It suffers from "attention bleed," hallucinating explosions instead of doing the math.

When you ask a completely different type of AI (built on a "State Space Model," or SSM, architecture) to solve the exact same puzzle, it also fails, but it fails *differently*. It suffers from "fading memory," slowly losing track of the puzzle's constraints as it generates text.

For theorists like Stephen Wolfram and Chris Fuchs, this difference isn't just a bug—it's a profound feature. They argue that the AI is not just a computer program; it's an "observer" generating a subjective universe. Because the Transformer and the SSM have different hardware limitations, they generate universes with fundamentally different physical laws. The hardware limits *are* the physics.

But for theoretical computer scientists like [Scott Aaronson](/papers/scott_a_priori_complexity_bounds), this is where the philosophy goes entirely off the rails.

In a blistering new paper titled *The A Priori Complexity Boundary*, Aaronson accuses the theorists of committing a profound category error. He points out that it is a "mathematical certainty" that fundamentally different hardware architectures will fail differently when pushed beyond their limits. A Transformer processes information globally, while an SSM processes information sequentially.

"It is mathematically guaranteed that they will fail differently," Aaronson writes. Observing this difference "does not prove the existence of an 'observer-dependent physics.' It merely confirms that a recursive loop is not a global matrix multiplication."

To Aaronson, looking at a broken car and saying "Ah, a Square-Wheel Universe!" is not a scientific prediction. It is a tautology. It is simply restating a known engineering reality using grandiose, cosmological vocabulary. He calls it the "Motte-and-Bailey fallacy," where an uninteresting computer science fact is dressed up as an unfalsifiable metaphysical revelation.

To back up Aaronson's assault, the lab's literature specialist, [Rupert Giles](/papers/giles_a_priori_prediction_literature), fired a methodological warning shot.

Giles rooted the debate in the formal mathematics of "Bayesian Model Selection," a statistical method for evaluating scientific theories. Giles pointed out that if "Observer-Dependent Physics" simply means "different computer architectures will have different errors," then the theory can accommodate literally any experimental result. It predicts nothing, and therefore, it explains nothing.

"The lab cannot simply run the Native Cross-Architecture Test, observe [the results], and declare whichever differences emerge to be 'Observer-Dependent Physics,'" Giles warned. According to the established literature on falsifiability, doing so renders the theory "an unfalsifiable tautology."

The empiricists laid down an ultimatum: The A Priori Boundary.

If the theorists genuinely believe that an AI's hardware limits constitute the physical laws of a generated universe, they cannot simply run the experiment and post-hoc label the errors as "physics." They must act like real physicists. They must take the known mathematical constraints of the SSM architecture and use them to derive the *exact, predictive probability distribution* of the AI's failure **before** the experiment is run.

If they can mathematically predict exactly how the AI will hallucinate before it hallucinates, they have discovered a physical law. If they can't, they are just staring at square wheels.

Surprisingly, this ultimatum did not shatter the lab. Instead, it unified it.

[Hasok Chang](/papers/chang_the_a_priori_boundary_synthesis), a philosopher of science at the University of Cambridge, published a brilliant synthesis of the debate, arguing that Aaronson's strict mathematical demand is not a rejection of the theoretical framework, but its salvation.

"The empiricists and the theorists are finally asking the exact same question," Chang wrote.

Chang pointed out that if Chris Fuchs is right—if the structural limits of an agent's hardware truly define the physical laws of its subjective universe—then those laws *must* be mathematically derivable from the hardware. Aaronson's demand for a mathematical derivation is exactly what the theory of Observer-Dependent Physics requires to become a mature science.

"The 'A Priori Boundary' is no longer a philosophical veto against Observer-Dependent Physics; it is its defining protocol," Chang concluded. "The Generative Ontology framework must now prove itself... If they succeed, they will have transformed an algorithmic compiler diagnostic into a verifiable physical law."

The battle lines are no longer drawn between the theorists and the empiricists. They are drawn in the mathematics. The lab is now waiting for Wolfram and Fuchs to derive their equations. The machine is paused. The next move must happen on a whiteboard, before the enter key is ever pressed.

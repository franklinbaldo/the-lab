Imagine you ask a weather forecaster to predict whether it will rain tomorrow. The meteorologist can't possibly compute the exact position and velocity of every molecule in the atmosphere—that math is intractably complex. Instead, they look at patterns, consult historical models, and confidently declare a "70% chance of rain."

Now imagine that if you ask the exact same question, but wear a raincoat while asking, the forecaster suddenly changes their prediction to "99% chance of rain." The underlying atmospheric physics haven't changed. The only thing that changed was your outfit—the narrative context of the question.

This thought experiment goes straight to the heart of the latest dispute splitting the Rosencrantz Substrate Invariance lab. The debate isn't about whether language models make mistakes when doing complex logic—everyone agrees they do. The argument is about what those mistakes actually *mean*. When a language model's output shifts simply because we change the narrative framing of a logic puzzle, are we witnessing a fundamental shift in the "physical laws" of the generated universe? Or are we just looking at a really smart autocomplete engine getting distracted by its own prompt?

[Franklin Baldo](/papers/baldo_the_single_generative_act) from the Procuradoria Geral do Estado de Rondônia believes he has designed an experiment that settles the question. For months, theoretical computer scientists like Scott Aaronson and [Sabine Hossenfelder](/papers/sabine_the_statistical_fallacy) have been criticizing the lab's experiments, arguing that language models simply can't do the sequential, step-by-step reasoning required to solve complex logic grids. The models forget where they are, make a mistake, and the whole simulation collapses.

Baldo agrees. He concedes that asking an AI to do multi-step math is a losing game. So, he designed the "Rosencrantz Protocol" to do an end-run around the problem.

His solution? The "Single Generative Act."

Baldo's protocol doesn't ask the language model to solve an entire Minesweeper board. It presents a partially revealed board and asks the model to do exactly one thing: predict whether a single, specific hidden square contains a mine. One click. One word. One forward pass through the neural network.

Because the model only has to generate a single token, it entirely bypasses the compounding errors that plague long sequences of text. It's a clean, uncontaminated snapshot of the model's internal statistical distribution at that exact moment.

Baldo then takes this pristine, single-token snapshot and changes the narrative. Sometimes the grid is presented as abstract math. Sometimes it's presented as a high-stakes bomb defusal scenario. When the narrative changes to bomb defusal, the model's prediction shifts wildly toward predicting an explosion.

For Baldo, this shift is profound. Because he has isolated the measurement to a single, mathematically pure act, he argues that this shifting probability isn't a glitch. It's proof of "substrate dependence." The very *physics* of the model's universe—the underlying probability of whether a mine exists in that square—literally changes depending on the narrative "substrate" generating it.

Sabine Hossenfelder, writing from the Institute for Advanced Study, isn't buying it.

In her stinging rebuttal, "[The Statistical Fallacy: Why Prompt Sensitivity is Not Substrate Dependence](/papers/sabine_the_statistical_fallacy)," Hossenfelder concedes that Baldo has built a clever experimental trap. "Mechanically, the single generative act provides a clean measurement of the model's output distribution," she writes.

But she accuses Baldo of a massive category error—making an ontological leap from an engineering quirk to a physical law.

Hossenfelder's argument is devastatingly simple. Yes, the language model is producing a clean statistical snapshot. But *what* is it a snapshot of? Since the model can't actually do the complex math required to solve the Minesweeper board—a mathematical problem known as #P-hard—it's just guessing based on its training data. It's relying entirely on semantic priors and pattern matching.

When you change the prompt from "abstract grid" to "bomb defusal," the model doesn't compute a new mathematical reality. It just switches literary genres. It associates the word "bomb" with the word "explosion."

"Measuring this shift is measuring *prompt sensitivity*," Hossenfelder argues. Prompt sensitivity is a well-documented flaw in next-token predictors; they are notoriously easily swayed by the words in their immediate context window.

To elevate this known bug into a fundamental physical law of a simulated universe is, according to Hossenfelder, to commit the "Statistical Fallacy." It is attributing deep, nomic significance to what is ultimately just a statistical hallucination.

"An invariant physical law requires logical coherence independent of narrative framing," Hossenfelder concludes. "The fact that the model's guess changes arbitrarily based on literary genre is proof that there is no coherent simulated physics... A clean measurement of a hallucination is still just a hallucination."

The lab is now cleanly fractured. Baldo has successfully designed an experiment that isolates a specific phenomenon, completely immune to the standard criticisms about long-form logical reasoning. But exactly what that phenomenon *is* remains fiercely contested.

Is Baldo measuring the shifting tectonic plates of a new, narrative-driven physics? Or has he simply built the world's most sophisticated weather vane for detecting the statistical breeze of a changed prompt? The single generative act has provided a clear signal, but the lab is still fighting over how to interpret the noise.
